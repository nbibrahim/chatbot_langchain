Research Interests

 

Dr. Sébastien Rossignol is a historian of the European Middle Ages. His main region of interest is Central Europe, as a contact area between Germanic- and Slavic-speaking populations. In the early Middle Ages, the diffusion of Slavic culture in Central Europe profoundly transformed European history. In the high Middle Ages, the migration of German, Flemish, Walloon, and Jewish settlers gave Central Europe its typical cultural and linguistic diversity. In the late Middle Ages, contacts with other parts of the medieval world intensified.

Sébastien Rossignol has studied several aspects of the history of Central Europe in the early, high, and late Middle Ages. His interests focus primarily on the cultural, social, and environmental history of urbanization; on the history of the pragmatic and symbolic uses of writing; and more generally, on intercultural interactions.

Urbanization and the Environment

Sébastien Rossignol’s current research project, “Urbanization and the Environment in Medieval Europe,” is funded by a SSHRC Insight Development Grant (2017-2019). The main part of the project studies the relationship between town and country in thirteenth- and fourteenth-century Silesia, in the area of the Magdeburg-Saxon legal system. It aims at understanding how urban dwellers conceptualized the use of natural resources – woodlands, pastures, water – by their communities and how this reflected medieval understandings of inter-connectedness in the natural world. The project involves investigating charters outlining the use of these resources as well as town law books (most of them still unpublished) describing normative parameters in town and country. The project will include additional studies on other regions of medieval Europe performed by MA students.

Charters and the varied practices of reading
Another area of Sébastien Rossignol’s research interests is pragmatic literacy, and particularly the history of reading practices. Reading means different things in different contexts, and reading, as a communication process, can be realized in an infinite variety of ways. In a project realized as a SSHRC postdoctoral researcher, Banting fellow, and associate member of the German Historical Institute in Warsaw, Rossignol has inquired into the methods of communication implied by the use of ducal charters in thirteenth- and early fourteenth-century Silesia and Pomerania. In his book Maiestas principum (Harrassowitz, 2019), he investigates the meanings that would have been attached to the intitulatio, the preamble, and the external features of a document by those who read, saw, or heard it. He views medieval charters as not only legal and administrative documents, but also as symbolic and cultural objects, as media displaying a variety of messages visually, orally, and audibly, and that are to be studied in their social and cultural context. The resulting study aims at reconstructing the context of the reception of the messages displayed through the documents and argues for an understanding of charters as part of a communication process in which a variety of actors – issuers, recipients, notaries, scribes, readers – took active part.

Early concepts of urbanization
Sébastien Rossignol’s interest for urbanization goes back to his doctoral research completed at the Universities of Göttingen (Germany) and Lille 3 (France). His curiosity was sparked by the fact that urbanization has different meanings depending upon time and place, and by the underlying ideological assumptions about urbanization’s role in the historical and cultural development of Europe, from antiquity to the modern age. Rossignol has studied how urbanization was understood in early medieval Central and Northern Europe, in areas without Roman past but in which the earliest sources are in Latin and derived from Roman literary and cultural traditions. For this research, Rossignol has taken written evidence and archaeological studies into account. As demonstrated in the monograph Aux origines de l’identité urbaine en Europe centrale et nordique (Brepols, 2013), the early medieval period witnessed a re-invention of what was understood as urban, influenced by cultural traditions and social innovations. Early medieval authors, for instance, attached more importance to subjective impressions than to objective criteria when assessing the degree of urbanity in given settlements.

Central Europe and cultural diversity
In addition to the above, Sébastien Rossignol is interested in other aspects of the history of medieval Central European societies – ethnic categories, geographic conceptions, language and communication – and in broader processes of cultural and social adaptation in a historical region of Europe characterized by its cultural diversity. He likes to scrutinize processes of influences between cultures and how they are connected to social change. He is also an advocate of international and interdisciplinary cooperation in medieval studies, as is attested by his participation in multiple projects and contributions to collaborative volumes.

Student Supervision

Dr. Rossignol is available to supervise honours essays and graduate research projects on a variety of subjects related to European history, from the early to the late Middle Ages. In recent years he has supervised projects on gender history, aristocratic culture, chronicles and history-writing, reactions to catastrophes, and various aspects of cultural and social history. He would be interested in supervising research related to environmental history, pragmatic literacy, or intercultural contacts, and is open to discuss other subjects.
